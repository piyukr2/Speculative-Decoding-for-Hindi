Morphological (UPOS) Analysis - Plain English Explanation
==========================================================

What this section is about:
----------------------------
This section asks: "When the draft model tries to predict the next word, does it do
better or worse depending on what *type* of word it's predicting?" To answer this,
every predicted token is tagged with its grammatical category (called a UPOS tag --
Universal Part-of-Speech tag), and then we check the acceptance rate for each category
separately.

The UPOS categories and what they mean:
-----------------------------------------
- ADP (Adpositions/Postpositions): Words like "in", "on", "of". In Hindi, these come
  after the noun (e.g., "ke", "mein", "par").

- AUX (Auxiliaries): Helper verbs like "is", "was", "has been". In Hindi, words like
  "hai", "tha", "hoga".

- PRON (Pronouns): Words like "he", "she", "it", "they" -- "woh", "yeh", "main" in Hindi.

- PUNCT (Punctuation): Commas, periods, question marks, etc.

- NOUN (Common Nouns): General words for things/concepts -- "ghar" (house), "kitab" (book).

- PROPN (Proper Nouns): Names of specific people, places, organizations -- "Delhi",
  "Narendra", "Google".

- VERB (Verbs): Action words -- "karna" (to do), "jana" (to go), "likhna" (to write).


What the results show:
-----------------------
The draft model's accuracy varies dramatically by word type:

1. ADP (100% accepted) and AUX (100% accepted):
   These are "function words" -- there are only a handful of them in Hindi, and which
   one to use is almost entirely determined by the surrounding sentence structure. For
   example, after a noun, the postposition is highly predictable. Since there are so few
   options, even a lightweight draft model can guess them perfectly.

2. PRON (75% accepted):
   Pronouns are also a small set, but in Hindi they change form based on gender and
   number (e.g., "woh" vs "wey"), so there's some ambiguity. The model gets it right
   3 out of 4 times.

3. PUNCT (58% accepted):
   Punctuation seems like it should be easy, but in Hindi, comma and period placement
   is less standardized than in English. The model gets it right a bit more than half
   the time.

4. NOUN (53% accepted):
   Common nouns are harder because there are thousands of them, and in Hindi they also
   carry case markers (suffixes that change based on grammatical role). So the model
   has to predict both the right word AND the right form of that word.

5. PROPN (29% accepted):
   Names are very hard to predict. They are "open class" -- new names appear all the
   time, they don't follow grammatical patterns, and which name appears depends heavily
   on context/topic. The model only gets about 1 in 3.5 right.

6. VERB (8% accepted):
   Verbs are the hardest by far. This is because Hindi verbs change form based on the
   subject's person (I/you/he), number (singular/plural), AND gender (masculine/feminine).
   For example, "to do" becomes "karta" (masc. sing.), "karti" (fem. sing.), "karte"
   (masc. plural). The draft model, being smaller and less capable, struggles to predict
   all these inflectional variations correctly. Only about 1 in 12 verb predictions is
   accepted.

The key takeaway:
-----------------
Simple, grammatically constrained words (function words) are easy for the draft model.
Complex, content-rich words -- especially verbs with Hindi's rich morphology -- are very
hard. This tells us that speculative decoding's efficiency depends heavily on the type
of text being generated. Passages with lots of verbs and proper nouns will see more
rejected drafts and slower performance.

Important caveat:
-----------------
The sample sizes for some categories are very small (only 3 AUX tokens, 4 PRON tokens,
8 ADP tokens). So the "perfect" scores for ADP and AUX might just be luck -- with only
3-8 examples, we can't be very confident. The results for PUNCT (83 tokens) and PROPN
(21 tokens) are more statistically reliable.
